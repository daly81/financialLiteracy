The decision making process

The decision-making process

  • Identify the problem.
  • Gather all the information you can and look for possible alternatives.
  • Consider consequences for each alternative.
  • Select the best course of action.
  • Evaluate the outcome.

Common decision-making strategies

Common decision-making strategies

Avoidance

Trying to avoid the worse possible outcome.

Compliance

following the expectations from your peers(or family members).

Desire

Ignoring any or all the risk involed by picking the option that will give you the highest pay off.

Security

The option the will offer the least risk and little return.

Spontaneity

Going with the first option you see with no consideration of the risks involved.

Risks associated with decision making

Risks associated with decision making

Income risk

How much job security do you have? A change in your career or loss of employment can lower your income and reduce your buying power.

Inflation risk

Rising prices cause lower buying power. For example, buying a house at the right time (when the market allows for lower prices) will give you more buying power.

Interest-rate risk

Anytime you are borrowing, or saving money the interest rate will a role. It will have an affect on how much it will cost you to borrow, or how much your will gain while saving.

Personal risks

Personal risks come in the form of health concerns, family issues, or anything that could jeopardize your decision.

Opportunity costs and the time value of money

Opportunity costs

Refer to the cost of your time, the cost of your effort, and the actual cost. Making tough decisions may involve one or more of these resources.

Financial opportunity costs

will come with a monetay value. For example, withdrawing some of your money from your savings account means you can no longer collect interest on that money.

Personal opportunity costs

Think of the trade off in the value of having a full time job. Working will mean lost time for leisure, but the trade off is work experience and an income. Losing leisure time, or time that could be spent with friends for family comes at a personal cost.

The Time value of money

Can be used to measure financial opportunity costs using interest calculations.

  • $1000 X .04 (4 %) X 1 year = $40. Over 10 years, that $40 a year saved at 4% would have a value of over $480 due to compound interest.
  • Spending $1,000 from a savings account paying 4 percent a year means an opportunity cost of $40 in lost interest.
